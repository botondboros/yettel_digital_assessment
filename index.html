Executive Summary

Yettel’s digital funnels are competitive but leaking — making friction removal the primary driver of both growth and NPS uplift

The two tasks

  • Task 1Scale Yepp — turn Yettel’s app-only, digital-first plan into a profitable acquisition engine rather than a leaky one.
  • Task 2Digital transformation — lift NPS and cut cost-per-transaction while moving digital & assisted sales from 20% to 50% by 2028.
What the data suggest: Task 1
Yepp’s problem is retention, not acquisition: activations hold steady (~3,100/mo) while net adds collapse from ~2,700 to ~600 and only 48% of ever-registered users remain active. The biggest lever is payment recovery, not marketing: 38% of churn is involuntary — these customers don’t want to leave, the system drops them when a monthly auto-charge fails (expired card, no funds, declined SCA) — and the Android cohort compounds it, churning near twice the iOS rate (9.2% vs 5.1%) on a platform holding 44% of the base. Churn is front-loaded in the first 90 days. Plugging the leak and reallocating spend beats buying more activations.
Where Yepp leaks — the lifetime funnel

Of 88,650 who ever registered, only 42,800 (48.3%) are active today — more than half spills out. The two upper leaks (onboarding, trial→paid) are roughly equal in size, but the bottom leak is the most fixable: a large share of it is involuntary churn from failed auto-charges, concentrated on Android. Plug the leaks before buying more registrations.

When Yepp leaks — the first-90-day cliff

Monthly churn is front-loaded — 11.2% in the first 90 days vs 3.1% after a year. Win the first quarter with onboarding, not discounts — the customer becomes ~3.6× stickier. This window decides LTV.

Usage & engagement — the product experience is degrading (Jun → May)

Every product-health metric moved the wrong way in a single cohort window: sessions and session length fell by a third, the crash rate nearly tripled (1.2% → 3.5%), pages load slower, the store rating slipped 4.2 → 3.9, and voice add-on attach dropped 44% → 34%. Degrading reliability is upstream of the churn — a crashing app and a falling rating feed the leak, and the decay is worst on Android.

Channel economics — the Campaign mis-allocation

Campaign is simultaneously the most expensive CPA and the worst retention — a retained customer costs ~13.5× what it does on Referral. Referral is the unicorn: cheapest and stickiest (84% conversion, 68% 6-month retention, 6,680 ARPU) — yet under-funded. A textbook mis-allocation: spend is flowing to the channel that returns the least.

Task 1 — Scale Yepp · five-point strategy

Plug the leak before buying growth

  • 1Payment recovery. Ship smart-retry timing, an automated dunning sequence, proactive card-update prompts and an alternative-payment fallback to win back failed auto-charges.
  • 2Android stabilization. Run a crash-triage and performance sprint on the Android build, with QA on low-end devices, to close the platform gap.
  • 3First-90-day onboarding. Redesign activation — guided first-run, early-value moments, habit nudges and a save-the-cancel flow through the first quarter.
  • 4Channel reallocation. Shift budget out of Campaign and Retail into Referral and Organic, and stand up a member-get-member referral engine.
  • 5In-app signup friction. Streamline the KYC + card-on-file step — document-scan pre-fill, deferred fields, a smoother SCA hand-off and instant eSIM by default.
What the data suggest: Task 2
Across the web Yettel is competitive on every lens: a close second after One on checkout friction, the SEO leader on content depth, and second on AI visibility, where it already owns the high-intent 0-Ft / eSIM / online-buying queries. In the app it holds the best-rated Android build of the three (4.1★) with the smallest cross-platform gap, yet its measured #1 complaint is auth / login (29% of negative reviews) plus a live Telenor→Yettel migration tail (15%) — and the app is still mostly self-service, with Yepp the only in-app acquisition. The Yettel digital channels nearly win the top of the funnel; the gap to 50% is conversion and friction, not visibility — fix checkout, auth/login and in-app commerce and the traffic Yettel already attracts converts.

Task 1 — Scale Yepp · the actions

Plug the leak before buying growth

  • 1Payment recovery (the involuntary-churn fix). Build an intelligent retry & recovery layer on top of the payment stack. How: turn on the payment gateway's smart-retry + network tokenization (e.g. Adyen, Stripe, or the incumbent PSP's dunning module) to re-attempt failed charges on pay-cycle-optimal days; subscribe to a card account-updater (Visa Account Updater / Mastercard ABU) so expired cards refresh automatically; drive card-update prompts and failed-charge nudges through a lifecycle-messaging tool (e.g. Braze / CleverTap) over push + SMS + email; add an alternative-payment fallback (mobile balance, direct debit, Apple Pay / Google Pay tokens). Who: a small payments squad — one backend engineer, the PSP integration owner and a lifecycle marketer, sponsored jointly by Finance and Digital. Org change: make involuntary churn a named, jointly-owned KPI with a weekly dunning-performance review. ▸ the cheapest, biggest retention lever · low–med effort · first ship 4–6 wks, full ~2 mo
  • 2Android stabilization. Make the Android build as stable as iOS. How: stand up crash + performance observability (Firebase Crashlytics + Play Console Android Vitals, plus an APM SDK such as Sentry or Embrace); run a crash-triage sprint on the top crash / ANR clusters and a cold-start + memory perf sprint; add a low-end-device test matrix (Firebase Test Lab / BrowserStack App Live) covering the cheap handsets common in the Yepp base; gate releases in CI on a crash-free-sessions threshold. Who: a dedicated Android squad (≈2 engineers + 1 QA) with an observability / SRE owner, plus a short external Android-performance consultant if in-house bandwidth is thin. Org change: crash-free rate becomes a hard release gate and an app on-call rotation is established. ▸ closes the platform churn + NPS gap · med–high effort · ~2–4 mo
  • 3First-90-day onboarding. Replace the drop-on-the-home-screen first run with a guided path to first value. How: build a 3–4 step guided first-run with an in-app onboarding tool (e.g. Appcues, Pendo or UserGuiding, or native coachmarks) that lands the user on a real first success — a completed top-up or first data session — rather than the dashboard; wire behaviour-triggered lifecycle messaging (Braze / CleverTap / Firebase) for a day-1 “finish your first top-up”, an “auto top-up is on” reassurance and a day-3–7 “you've used X GB” value moment; add a save-the-cancel interceptor that catches port-out / cancel taps with a one-tap “fix my issue” or retention offer; instrument the whole first-90-day funnel in a product-analytics tool (Amplitude / Mixpanel / GA4) so every step's drop-off is visible and A/B-testable. Who: a growth / onboarding squad — a product manager, a mobile engineer, a UX designer and a lifecycle marketer, with analytics support. Org change: a “first-90-day activation” cohort metric on the exec dashboard and standing A/B-test discipline. ▸ the window that sets LTV — churn is front-loaded · med effort · ~2–3 mo
  • 4Channel reallocation. Spend on the channels that retain, not the ones that merely acquire. How: stand up a member-get-member referral engine (a platform like Mention Me / Talkable / Friendbuy, or a custom build on the existing CRM) with unique codes, double-sided rewards and fraud controls; shift budget out of Campaign and Retail line items into Referral and Organic / ASO; model channel-level cost-per-6-month-retained in the BI stack (data warehouse + Looker / Power BI) and let retained value — not CPA — drive allocation, with source tracked through to 6-month retention. Who: a growth marketer + a BD owner for referral and a data analyst for the channel-economics model, with finance sign-off on budget moves. Org change: media budget governed monthly against cost-per-retained; referral becomes a named channel with its own owner. ▸ large efficiency gap left on the table · low effort · ~1 mo to reallocate, then ongoing
  • 5In-app signup friction. Cut the front-loaded KYC + card friction that gates acquisition and seeds later payment failures. How: integrate document-scan + liveness eKYC (e.g. Onfido / Veriff / Sumsub / IDnow) to auto-fill identity fields from an ID photo instead of manual typing; defer non-essential fields to after activation; replace the redirect SCA with an in-app 3-D Secure 2 biometric step-up; default to instant eSIM provisioning via the carrier entitlement server so the line is live immediately; pre-fill the card step with tokenized Apple Pay / Google Pay. Methodology

What’s measured, what’s estimated — stated plainly

The scoring formula and signal rubric, and the honest line between measured data (friction steps, iOS + Android store metrics, 717 classified Play reviews) and estimates (GEO probe, in-app friction).

1 · The task

Comparability comes from holding the task identical across all three operators. Any deviation is recorded in notes.

    2 · The score

    Each operator starts at 100. Every step subtracts the friction observed at that step; bonuses add back. Lower final = more friction. The number is a relative friction index for one task — not an absolute quality rating, and not comparable across different tasks.

    final = 100 + Σ stepΔ  |  stepΔ = Σ(signal penalties) + Σ(bonuses) at that step

    3 · Signal rubric

    Every step delta is built from these observed signals — so each number traces to something seen, not a feeling. Weights reflect impact on the digital→assisted boundary: the two heaviest are telco-specific.

    4 · How these three were measured

    Not a template — this is the actual run behind the numbers.

    • MethodEach operator's handset→checkout journey walked once with a Playwright (Chromium) script and confirmed manually, in a fresh incognito session with cookies cleared.
    • Reference configiPhone 16 128 GB · 24-month postpaid (voice + data) · number port-in · new consumer · Hungary. Held constant across all three.
    • CaptureA screenshot saved at each of the 8 journey stages (shots/<key>-0<step>.png); each step delta scored from the §3 signals against that screenshot.
    • Stop pointStopped at the checkout gate — no real identity/credit submission and no signature was completed.
    • Shared normIdentity confirmed by document at courier handover is common to all three, so it is treated as an industry norm and not scored as a differentiator.
    • DatesYettel & Magyar Telekom walked 2026-06-18; One walked 2026-06-19.

    5 · SEO read — how

    An observational, single-pass read (2026-06-19), not a Lighthouse run. From search-visible evidence — what each domain ranks for, content depth, indexed-page behaviour — five dimensions are rated 0–100 and averaged. Precise performance / Core Web Vitals are deferred to PageSpeed Insights (linked per domain on the SEO tab), because the live crawl can't be reproduced here.

    • DimensionsBranded SERP ownership · category-keyword reach · content depth / topical authority · technical hygiene (observed) · brand-equity continuity.
    • SourcesLive search across branded, category and product queries (HU + EN); operator domains; NMHH and tech-press references.
    • Not measured hereLighthouse scores, Core Web Vitals, exact indexed-page counts, backlink/DA metrics — use PageSpeed / Search Console / a backlink tool for those.

    6 · GEO probe — how

    GEO = generative-engine optimization: how likely a brand is to be surfaced and cited in AI-assistant answers. The probe is anchored to the same purchase as the Friction tab — iPhone 16 128 GB on a 24-month plan — not generic “best operator” questions, so it reads the visibility that actually drives this sale. Single-model, single-pass (Claude grounded with web search, 2026-06-19); a directional read, not a measured multi-model share.

    operator GEO = average of the 10 per-question answer-presence scores (0–100)
    • Questions10 purchase-intent prompts (HU + EN), all for iPhone 16 128 GB + plan: which operator, 0 Ft up-front, cheapest instalment, 24-month comparison, unlimited-data tariff, eSIM, English buy, number port-in, order-online, best value.
    • Cell score 0–100Per question, each operator’s answer-presence is estimated from two observable proxies: does it have a dedicated, crawlable, citable page for that intent, and does its positioning match the query. Higher = more likely an assistant surfaces and quotes it.
    • Brand dilutionOne is marked down where it is still cited as “Vodafone” after the 2025 rebrand (most visible on the English query); Yettel carries a small residual “Telenor” drag.
    • LeadsPer question, the operator with the highest answer-presence is the “lead” for that query. Across the 10 questions the leads split 5–5 between Yettel and Telekom; One takes none — the split shown on the Executive Summary GEO chart.
    • CaveatSingle model + single pass, scored from live search evidence — not a measured multi-model run. A production score would average several models over many runs (as the standalone live tool does).

    7 · App read — how

    The App tab reads the store layer directly on both platforms — Apple App Store (iOS) and Google Play (Android), Hungarian listings: rating, rating count, download size / install band and package id, captured 2026-06-19, each linked to verify.

    • Review-theme mining (run)Not just described — it was executed: 717 negative reviews (rating ≤ 3) were pulled from Google Play across the three apps and classified into themes by an LLM (Claude). This is the source of the measured complaint mix on the App and Executive tabs (e.g. Yettel auth / login 29%, Telekom billing 39%, One auth 40%).
    • Play-only · varianceThe App Store review fetch failed at run time, so the theme percentages are Google-Play-only and carry ±classifier variance — stated as such wherever they appear.
    • Not run hereThe remaining layers — in-app funnel analytics and a task-based in-app UX walk — are laid out as method only. They need in-app event data (App Store Connect / Play Console / Firebase) and a device harness (Maestro / Appium).

    8 · Yepp diagnostics — source

    The Yepp tab summarises the supplied Yepp performance workbook (monthly overview, sales funnel, customer-status, tenure cohorts, churn breakdown, usage/engagement, acquisition channels, campaign history).     9 · Diagnosis vs recommendation — kept separate

    Every diagnostic tab — Yepp, App, Friction, SEO, GEO — states findings only. All prioritisation, actions and roadmaps are consolidated in the Executive Summary and the Actions tab, so the deck has a single source of truth and no competing or contradictory to-do lists. Charts use one consistent palette throughout: Yettel = green (the same green also flags a measured signal), Telekom = magenta, One = purple; amber / orange is reserved for friction and for estimated (vs measured) values.
